Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript, **"IS6-family transposons drive carbapenemase mobilisation in *Klebsiella pneumoniae*: a genome-wide analysis of 492 clinical isolates"**, for consideration as an Article in *Nature Communications*.

**Scientific significance.** Carbapenem-resistant *Klebsiella pneumoniae* (CRKP) is classified by the WHO as a Priority 1 critical pathogen, yet the mechanistic drivers of carbapenemase gene spread remain incompletely understood at the genomic scale. Our study addresses this gap through a systematic analysis of 492 publicly deposited complete genomes spanning 2015–2025, integrating IS-element burden profiling, AMR gene detection, and locus-level flanking-context characterisation across 2,985 IS–AMR genomic pairs.

**Key findings.**

1. **IS6-family burden is the dominant predictor of carbapenem resistance** (AUC = 0.976; 95 % CI 0.963–0.988), outperforming total IS load and IS*26* copy number as individual features. Among resistant genomes, the median IS6-family copy count is 8-fold higher than in susceptible controls (Mann–Whitney *U*, *p* < 10⁻³⁰; Cohen's *d* = 2.14).

2. **Composite transposons account for 58 % of IS–AMR flanking architectures**, with inverted IS*26* pairs flanking *bla*KPC-2/3 in 91 % of cases — consistent with a unit-transposition model for carbapenemase mobilisation.

3. **A significant temporal escalation** was detected between the 2015–2019 and 2020–2025 cohorts: the proportion of genomes carrying ≥ 2 IS6-family copies rose from 43 % to 71 % (Fisher's exact OR = 3.27; 95 % CI 2.01–5.32; *p* = 8.4 × 10⁻⁷), and the IS6 burden AUC improved from 0.958 to 0.991 in the 2025 sub-cohort — suggesting ongoing IS-mediated genomic adaptation.

4. **Five genomes carry dual carbapenemase classes** (KPC + NDM co-carriage), all with composite transposon architectures, identifying a high-priority resistance profile for clinical surveillance.

**Suitability for *Nature Communications*.** This work is of broad interest across microbiology, infectious disease, genomics, and public health. The findings provide mechanistic evidence linking IS6-family transposon accumulation to carbapenem resistance spread, with direct implications for surveillance target prioritisation and resistance evolution modelling. The pipeline and all processed data are fully open (MIT licence; code: https://github.com/jiayu6954-sudo/amr-genomics-analysis).

This manuscript has not been submitted elsewhere and all authors approved the final version. The authors have no competing interests to declare.
